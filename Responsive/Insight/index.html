Schwab Equity Ratings® and the general buy/hold/sell guidance are not personal recommendations for any particular investor or client and do not take into account the financial, investment or other objectives or needs of, and may not be suitable for, any particular investor or client. Investors and clients should consider Schwab Equity Ratings as only a single factor in making their investment decision while taking into account the current market environment.

Schwab Equity Ratings® are assigned to approximately 3,000 of the largest (by market capitalization) stocks issued by companies headquartered or incorporated in the U.S. and, in some cases, certain foreign countries where companies typically locate or incorporate for operational or tax reasons. Stocks are rated using a scale of A, B, C, D and F. Schwab's outlook is that A-rated stocks, on average, will strongly outperform and F-rated stocks, on average, will strongly underperform the equities market over the next 12 months. Each of the approximately 3,000 stocks rated in the Schwab Equity Ratings universe is given a composite score derived from its ratings on several research factors. The universe of rated stocks is then divided into two market capitalization categories: large-cap (the 800 largest stocks by market value) and small-cap (the remaining stocks, approximately 2,000). These two categories are further divided into ten economic sectors. The assignment of a final Schwab Equity Rating depends on how well a given stock's composite score ranks, based on percentile ranking, against the composite scores of other stocks in the same capitalization group and sector. The result is a within-capitalization-group, within-sector (or cap-neutral, sector-neutral) percentile rank. The stocks with the lowest percentile rankings represent the best composite scores and correspond to the best letter grades.
